Chapter 22a: The Lymphatic System

Structured Academic Portfolio Document

Function of the Lymphatic System

  1. Maintain Blood Volume and Fluid Balance: Returns approximately 3 liters of excess interstitial fluid and leaked plasma proteins to the bloodstream daily.
  2. Transport Lipids: Lacteals in the small intestine absorb dietary fats and fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K).
  3. Immunity and Defense: Houses lymphocytes and filters pathogens from lymph before it returns to circulation.

Anatomy Overview

Lymph
  • Fluid similar to plasma but lower in protein.
  • Contains water, electrolytes, lymphocytes, waste products, and sometimes fats (chyle).
Lymphatic Vessels
  • Begin as blind-ended capillaries in tissues.
  • Return lymph to the bloodstream at the subclavian veins.
  • Flow is one-way.
Lymphatic Tissues and Organs
  • Lymph nodes
  • Thymus
  • Spleen
  • Lymphoid nodules

Lymphatic Vessels

  • Lymphatic capillaries are highly permeable.
  • Collect interstitial fluid and pathogens.
  • Contain valves to prevent backflow.

Mechanics of Lymph Flow

  • Skeletal muscle contraction
  • Respiratory movements
  • One-way valves
  • Smooth muscle contraction in vessel walls

Lymphocytes

  • B cells – mature in bone marrow
  • T cells – mature in thymus
  • Natural Killer (NK) cells

Lymphoid Nodules

  • Aggregations of lymphocytes.
  • Found in mucous membranes (MALT).
  • Example: Tonsils, Peyer’s patches.

Lymph Nodes

  • Filter lymph.
  • Contain macrophages and lymphocytes.
  • Common sites: cervical, axillary, inguinal regions.

Thymus

  • Site of T-cell maturation.
  • Most active in childhood.
  • Involutes with age.

Spleen

  • Largest lymphatic organ.
  • Filters blood (not lymph).
  • Removes old red blood cells.
  • Contains red pulp and white pulp.
