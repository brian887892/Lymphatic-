🛡️ defence mechanisms · 2026

100 short‑answer questions (5–10 marks each) · full answers inline · nursing focus
1
8 marksDefine defence mechanisms. Explain their key characteristics.
Answer: Defence mechanisms are unconscious psychological strategies used to reduce anxiety, internal conflict, and emotional distress. Characteristics: (1) operate unconsciously, (2) protect the ego, (3) reduce anxiety, (4) distort reality to varying degrees.
2
5 marksWho first described defence mechanisms, and who later expanded the work?
Answer: Sigmund Freud first described them; Anna Freud later expanded and systematised the concept of defence mechanisms.
3
7 marksWhy is understanding defence mechanisms important in nursing practice?
Answer: Nurses care for patients facing chronic illness, trauma, pain, loss. Understanding defence mechanisms helps nurses avoid misinterpreting behaviour, improve therapeutic communication, reduce conflict, promote coping, and support mental health.
4
6 marksList and briefly describe the four levels of defence mechanisms.
Answer: (1) Primitive – highly distorted reality (e.g., psychosis‑level denial). (2) Immature – common in adolescents (e.g., acting out). (3) Neurotic – common in adults (e.g., repression). (4) Mature – healthy coping (e.g., sublimation).
5
5 marksGive an example of a primitive defence and explain why it is considered primitive.
Answer: Psychosis‑level denial – the individual completely rejects reality despite clear evidence. It is primitive because reality is extremely distorted, often seen in severe psychiatric conditions.
6
5 marksWhat is the difference between suppression and repression? Provide an example of each.
Answer: Suppression = conscious decision to postpone dealing with an emotion (e.g., “I’ll think about this after surgery”). Repression = unconscious blocking of painful thoughts (e.g., cannot recall traumatic ICU experience).
7
8 marksDefine denial. Describe appropriate nursing interventions for a patient using denial.
Answer: Denial = refusing to accept reality/facts. Nursing: do not confront aggressively; provide factual information gradually; allow time for adjustment; support emotional processing; avoid reinforcing false beliefs but gently introduce reality.
8
8 marksExplain projection with a clinical example. How should the nurse respond?
Answer: Projection = attributing one’s own unacceptable feelings to another. Example: patient afraid of surgery says, “You nurses seem nervous about my condition.” Nursing: respond calmly, do not become defensive, clarify feelings gently (“it sounds like you might be feeling anxious”).
9
7 marksDefine displacement and give a typical healthcare example. What is the nursing approach?
Answer: Displacement = redirecting emotions (often anger) from a threatening target to a safer one. Example: patient angry at diagnosis shouts at the nurse. Nursing: do not personalise anger, acknowledge underlying feeling (“you seem really upset”), maintain professional boundaries.
10
6 marksDescribe regression. Why might a postoperative patient show regression?
Answer: Regression = reverting to earlier developmental behaviour under stress. Postoperative patient may become dependent, childlike, demand help with basic tasks – a way to cope with vulnerability.
11
8 marksHow should a nurse support a patient who is regressing after surgery?
Answer: Encourage independence gradually (praising small efforts), provide reassurance, avoid fostering dependency (e.g., by doing everything for them). Set gentle expectations while validating their need for support.
12
6 marksDefine rationalization. Provide an example from a patient with a chronic illness.
Answer: Rationalization = creating logical excuses for illogical or unacceptable behaviour. Example: smoker with COPD says, “Everyone dies anyway, so why quit?”
13
7 marksWhat nursing interventions are useful for a patient using rationalization?
Answer: Explore ambivalence (gently highlight discrepancies), use motivational interviewing, avoid arguing or ridiculing the excuse. Help patient see the real reasons behind behaviour.
14
5 marksExplain reaction formation and give a clinical example.
Answer: Reaction formation = behaving opposite to one’s true feelings. Example: patient who is terrified of dying becomes overly cheerful, makes jokes after terminal diagnosis.
15
6 marksHow can a nurse assess hidden distress when a patient displays reaction formation?
Answer: Observe non‑verbal cues (restlessness, sleep changes, withdrawal). Create a safe, private space and gently invite feelings: “sometimes people put on a brave face but feel very scared inside.”
16
7 marksDefine sublimation and explain why it is considered a mature defence.
Answer: Sublimation = channeling unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable activities. Mature because it transforms negative energy into constructive behaviour (e.g., anger → joining a support group, art, sport).
17
5 marksWhat is intellectualization? Provide an example.
Answer: Intellectualization = focusing excessively on facts, logic, and technical details to avoid emotional distress. Example: after a frightening diagnosis, patient talks only about lab values, statistics, and treatment protocols.
18
8 marksDescribe how a nurse can gently explore emotions with a patient using intellectualization.
Answer: First acknowledge the patient’s knowledge (“you’ve really researched this”). Then ask open-ended emotion-focused questions: “I wonder how all this information makes you feel inside?” or “what’s the hardest part to cope with?”
19
5 marksDefine identification and give a nursing example.
Answer: Identification = adopting characteristics of someone admired. Example: a child with chronic illness wants to become a nurse or doctor.
20
5 marksWhat is compensation? Provide a healthcare example.
Answer: Compensation = overachieving in one area to offset real or perceived weakness. Example: an amputee becomes an outstanding student to cope with body image concerns.
21
6 marksDifferentiate maladaptive and adaptive defence mechanisms, giving two examples of each.
Answer: Maladaptive = persistent distortion, hinders coping (e.g., denial, projection). Adaptive = flexible, reduces anxiety constructively (e.g., sublimation, humour).
22
5 marksList four defence mechanisms commonly seen in oncology nursing.
Answer: Denial, intellectualization, rationalization, and sometimes reaction formation.
23
7 marksDescribe the defence mechanism “splitting”. In which disorder is it frequently observed, and what is the nursing role?
Answer: Splitting = viewing people or situations as all good or all bad. Common in borderline personality disorder. Nursing role: maintain consistent team communication, set firm boundaries, avoid taking sides.
24
6 marksIdentify defence mechanisms common in critical care nursing. Why are they frequent in that setting?
Answer: Dissociation, repression, denial. Critical care involves life‑threatening events, high stress – these defences protect the mind from overwhelming trauma.
25
8 marksWhat defence mechanisms are typical in paediatric nursing? Which therapeutic approaches are recommended?
Answer: Regression, fantasy, acting out. Approaches: use play therapy, involve parents, provide reassurance, create a safe, predictable environment.
26
6 marksList common defence mechanisms in geriatric nursing and explain why they may appear.
Answer: Denial of memory loss, rationalization, withdrawal. They may arise from fear of dependency, loss of autonomy, cognitive decline.
27
7 marksExplain how nurses themselves use defence mechanisms and why self‑awareness matters.
Answer: Nurses may use emotional detachment (isolation), humour, suppression. Self‑awareness prevents compassion fatigue, countertransference, burnout, and promotes therapeutic use of self.
28
5 marksGive two examples of adaptive defence mechanisms nurses can encourage in patients.
Answer: Sublimation (creative/active outlet), humour, suppression (temporary delay), altruism (helping others).
29
5 marksList three therapeutic communication “do’s” when a patient uses defence mechanisms.
Answer: Use empathy, reflect feelings, maintain neutrality, provide safe environment.
30
5 marksWhat communication “avoid” actions are essential when handling defence mechanisms?
Answer: Avoid arguing with denial, taking projection personally, reinforcing regression, confronting aggressively.
31
8 marksCase: 45‑year‑old post‑MI says “it’s just indigestion, I’ll be back at work tomorrow.” Identify the defence and outline a full nursing plan.
Answer: Defence = denial. Plan: provide simple, factual education about MI; discuss risk factors; allow him to express fears gradually; involve family; follow up to monitor compliance; do not force acceptance.
32
7 marksDifferentiate between projection and displacement with clear examples.
Answer: Projection = attributing one’s own feeling to another (e.g., “you are nervous” → patient is nervous). Displacement = redirecting emotion to a safer target (e.g., angry at doctor, shouts at nurse).
33
5 marksWhy should a nurse not confront denial aggressively?
Answer: Denial often protects the patient from overwhelming anxiety. Aggressive confrontation may increase resistance, damage trust, and cause psychological harm. Gradual, supportive education is more effective.
34
6 marksWhat is the role of the nurse when a patient uses reaction formation (e.g., exaggerated cheerfulness)?
Answer: Watch for hidden distress, do not directly challenge the cheerfulness, provide a safe space, gently reflect (“sometimes people put on a brave face, it’s okay to feel scared”).
35
6 marksDescribe the nursing intervention for a patient using intellectualization.
Answer: Acknowledge the factual knowledge, then gently invite emotions: “you really understand the medical details – how is your heart handling all this?” Avoid dismissing the intellectual effort.
36
8 marksExplain the concept of “splitting” in psychiatric nursing and the importance of team consistency.
Answer: Splitting = patient categorizes staff as “all good” or “all bad”, causing staff conflict. Team consistency (same rules, same approach) and regular communication prevent staff splitting and maintain therapeutic milieu.
37
7 marksWhat is the difference between identification and imitation? How can identification be therapeutic?
Answer: Identification is unconscious adopting of admired traits; imitation is conscious copying. Therapeutic when patient identifies with positive role models (e.g., a coping role).
38
6 marksDefine altruism and explain why it is considered an adaptive defence mechanism.
Answer: Altruism = selfless concern for others, often channelling own anxiety into helping. Adaptive because it strengthens social bonds and provides meaning.
39
6 marksList the three vital centres regulated by the medulla oblongata (bonus cross‑reference) but in context of stress and defence, how can physiological and psychological defences interact?
Answer: (Though not in original doc, accept cardiac, respiratory, vasomotor centres). Psychological defences may reduce emotional arousal, thereby influencing sympathetic/parasympathetic balance.
40
10 marksDiscuss the nurse’s role in supporting a patient transitioning from maladaptive to adaptive coping. Provide examples.
Answer: Role: model adaptive coping; reinforce efforts (e.g., joining support group – sublimation); gently challenge maladaptive patterns without shaming; teach problem‑solving; refer to counselling. Example: move from denial (maladaptive) to information‑seeking (intellectualization) then to emotional expression.
41
5 marksWhat is the purpose of defence mechanisms according to Freudian theory?
Answer: To protect the ego from anxiety arising from conflict between id impulses and superego demands, or from external threats.
42
7 marksExplain how defence mechanisms can become maladaptive.
Answer: When used rigidly, persistently, and distort reality excessively, they prevent effective problem‑solving, damage relationships, and hinder emotional growth (e.g., chronic denial of illness).
43
5 marksGive an example of humour as a mature defence in a healthcare setting.
Answer: A patient awaiting surgery makes a light‑hearted joke about hospital food – releases tension without denying fear, maintains connection with staff.
44
8 marksHow can a nurse assess whether a defence mechanism is adaptive or maladaptive?
Answer: Look at flexibility, duration, reality distortion, impact on functioning. Adaptive: temporary, reduces anxiety, allows eventual coping. Maladaptive: rigid, long‑term, worsens situation, blocks growth.
45
6 marksDefine dissociation. In which nursing area is it commonly seen?
Answer: Dissociation = temporary detachment from reality, identity, or memory. Common in critical care nursing (trauma patients).
46
5 marksWhat is the difference between denial and avoidance?
Answer: Denial = unconscious refusal to accept reality. Avoidance = consciously staying away from situations that provoke anxiety.
47
7 marksDescribe how a nurse would handle a patient who projects hostility onto them.
Answer: Stay calm, do not react defensively, acknowledge the emotion behind the projection: “you seem really upset; it must be hard.” Explore source of distress without agreeing with false attribution.
48
6 marksList three signs that a patient might be using repression.
Answer: Gaps in memory about stressful events; becoming anxious when reminded of certain topics; vague somatic complaints without clear cause.
49
7 marksExplain the term “countertransference” and how it relates to nurses’ defence mechanisms.
Answer: Countertransference = nurse’s unconscious emotional reactions to a patient, often based on nurse’s own unresolved conflicts. Defence mechanisms (e.g., detachment) may be a sign of countertransference; self‑awareness is essential.
50
8 marksCase: A patient with a new stoma refuses to look at it or learn self‑care. Identify defence and nursing interventions.
Answer: Denial. Interventions: build trust, provide gradual exposure, involve stoma therapist, allow expression of disgust/fear, reinforce small steps (e.g., just looking).
51
5 marksWhat is the difference between primitive and immature defences?
Answer: Primitive = severe reality distortion (psychotic). Immature = common in adolescents/ some adults, less reality distortion but still problematic (acting out, projection).
52
6 marksDefine “acting out” and give an example from an adolescent patient.
Answer: Acting out = expressing unconscious feelings through behaviour rather than words. Example: teen with family conflict becomes verbally aggressive toward nurse.
53
8 marksHow can a nurse use the concept of defence mechanisms to enhance therapeutic communication?
Answer: By recognising the defence, nurse can tailor responses: not confronting denial, gently exploring feelings behind intellectualization, validating emotions under reaction formation, thus building trust and encouraging healthier coping.
54
5 marksWhat is the “ego” and why is it relevant to defence mechanisms?
Answer: The ego is the reality‑based part of psyche that mediates between id, superego, and external world. Defence mechanisms protect the ego from overwhelming anxiety.
55
7 marksExplain how defence mechanisms can be both protective and problematic in healthcare.
Answer: Protective: shield from acute distress, buy time to adjust. Problematic: if persistent, they prevent acceptance, treatment adherence, and emotional processing (e.g., long‑term denial worsens prognosis).
56
7 marksDescribe the defence mechanism “undoing” (briefly, as an extra). Can you relate it to any clinical behaviour?
Answer: Undoing = attempting to negate a previous thought/act by performing a contrary behaviour. Example: patient with aggressive impulses becomes excessively apologetic or helpful.
57
5 marksWhat defence mechanism might a patient use if they constantly make jokes about a serious illness? Is it adaptive?
Answer: Humour (mature defence) – can be adaptive if it relieves tension and connects with others, but may mask distress if used rigidly.
58
6 marksDifferentiate intellectualization from rationalization.
Answer: Intellectualization = focusing on abstract facts to avoid emotion. Rationalization = creating plausible excuses for behaviour. Both are neurotic but intellectualization avoids feeling, rationalization justifies action.
59
7 marksCase: A patient who is told they need dialysis says “I’ll just take better care of myself and won’t need it.” Identify the defence and discuss nursing response.
Answer: Denial. Response: provide clear, simple education about kidney function; explore fears; do not argue; involve family; reinforce that dialysis can be life‑saving.
60
10 marksDiscuss how a nurse’s awareness of their own defence mechanisms can improve patient care and reduce burnout.
Answer: Recognising personal use of detachment, humour, or suppression helps nurse engage authentically, avoid cynicism, and seek support. Self‑awareness reduces compassion fatigue, improves empathy, and models healthy coping.
61
6 marksDefine fantasy as a defence mechanism and give a paediatric example.
Answer: Fantasy = escaping unpleasant reality by imagining. Example: child in hospital pretends they are a superhero.
62
6 marksWhat is “isolation of affect”? How might it present in a patient?
Answer: Separating an idea from the emotion attached to it. Patient describes traumatic event without any feeling, as if reporting facts.
63
5 marksHow can defence mechanisms influence treatment adherence?
Answer: Denial or rationalization may lead to non‑adherence (e.g., “I don’t really need medication”). Understanding helps nurse address underlying fears.
64
7 marksCase: A newly diagnosed diabetic patient says “I can’t have diabetes, I feel fine.” Which defence? Nursing plan?
Answer: Denial. Plan: educate gently about diabetes, allow time, involve diabetic educator, discuss consequences without alarm, support gradual acceptance.
65
5 marksWhat does “support the transition from maladaptive to adaptive coping” mean in practice?
Answer: Helping the patient replace persistent, harmful defences (e.g., denial) with healthier strategies (e.g., seeking information, expressing emotion).
66
6 marksList two defence mechanisms that involve distortion of reality and two that involve avoidance.
Answer: Distortion: denial, projection. Avoidance: repression, suppression (conscious).
67
7 marksExplain the term “therapeutic use of self” in the context of defence mechanisms.
Answer: Nurse uses own personality, empathy, and self‑awareness to create a healing relationship, recognising and responding to patient’s defences without personal reaction.
68
5 marksWhat is the difference between identification and introjection?
Answer: Identification = adopting traits of another. Introjection = absorbing an external quality/object into oneself (more primitive, e.g., adopting values of an abuser).
69
8 marksCase: A patient who lost a leg in an accident says “I’ll walk again in a month, I just need to rest.” Identify defence and nursing approach.
Answer: Denial. Approach: allow hope but gently educate about rehabilitation timeline; involve physiotherapy early to show realistic progress; support grieving.
70
6 marksWhat is the potential harm of a nurse consistently using emotional detachment (isolation) as a defence?
Answer: May lead to compassion fatigue, burnout, loss of empathy, and depersonalised care.
71
5 marksDefine compensation and give an example unrelated to illness.
Answer: Emphasising strength to hide weakness. Example: short person becomes assertive leader.
72
6 marksWhat is the defence mechanism of “turning against self”?
Answer: Directing negative feelings toward oneself instead of others. May present as self‑blame or self‑harm.
73
7 marksHow might a nurse respond to a patient using displacement in a busy ward?
Answer: Remain calm, avoid taking it personally, later address the underlying emotion: “it seems you’re really angry – would you like to talk about what’s upsetting you?”
74
5 marksWhy is it important not to argue with rationalization?
Answer: Arguing makes patient defensive and reinforces the rationalization. Gentle exploration of ambivalence is more productive.
75
7 marksDescribe how a nurse can encourage sublimation in an angry patient.
Answer: Suggest physical activities, creative outlets, or joining a peer support group to channel anger constructively.
76
8 marksExplain the relationship between defence mechanisms and post‑traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).
Answer: In PTSD, repression and dissociation may occur during trauma, later manifesting as intrusive memories. Defence mechanisms are initial attempts to cope with overwhelming experience.
77
5 marksWhat is the main difference between suppression and denial?
Answer: Suppression = conscious postponement; denial = unconscious refusal to accept reality.
78
6 marksList three signs that a patient may be using intellectualization.
Answer: Excessive focus on medical facts, avoids emotion words, speaks in detached technical language.
79
7 marksCase: A patient awaiting biopsy says “I’m not worried, statistics show 90% are benign.” Identify defence and appropriate nursing reply.
Answer: Intellectualization. Reply: “It’s good you understand the stats – and it’s also normal to feel anxious while waiting.”
80
8 marksDiscuss the nurse’s role when a family member uses defence mechanisms (e.g., denial about a patient’s prognosis).
Answer: Show empathy, provide honest information gradually, allow questions, support family meetings, refer to counselling if needed. Do not force acceptance.
81
5 marksDefine ego and superego briefly.
Answer: Ego = realistic mediator; superego = internalised moral standards.
82
6 marksName two defence mechanisms that involve blaming others.
Answer: Projection, acting out (may blame others for own behaviour).
83
7 marksHow can a nurse help a patient move from reaction formation to genuine expression?
Answer: Provide safe, non‑judgmental space; reflect that many people feel scared even when they smile; wait and listen.
84
5 marksWhat is the “id” in Freudian theory?
Answer: The primitive, instinctual part of the psyche seeking immediate gratification.
85
8 marksCase: A patient with heart failure continues eating high‑salt foods, saying “a little won’t hurt.” Defence? Nursing intervention?
Answer: Rationalization. Intervention: explore understanding, use motivational interviewing, discuss consequences gently, involve dietitian.
86
6 marksWhat is the difference between identification and sublimation?
Answer: Identification = adopting traits of another. Sublimation = channelling impulse into acceptable behaviour.
87
5 marksName two defence mechanisms common in patients with terminal illness.
Answer: Denial, intellectualization, bargaining (reaction formation).
88
7 marksHow can defence mechanisms affect the grieving process?
Answer: Denial may delay mourning; intellectualization may block necessary emotional work. Adaptive defences (sublimation, humour) can help integrate loss.
89
6 marksExplain the term “alliance” in terms of working with patient defences.
Answer: Building a trusting relationship so patient feels safe enough to lower defences and explore difficult feelings.
90
7 marksCase: A patient says “the doctors are wrong, I’m going to get a second opinion that says I’m fine.” Defence? Nursing response?
Answer: Denial. Response: support patient’s right to seek information, but gently ask “what would feel manageable if the second opinion is the same?”
91
5 marksWhat is the main purpose of the ego?
Answer: To mediate between id impulses, superego demands, and external reality, using defence mechanisms to manage anxiety.
92
5 marksDefine “undoing” as a defence mechanism.
Answer: Attempting to symbolically cancel out an unacceptable thought or act by doing the opposite.
93
8 marksExplain the nursing role when a patient uses multiple defence mechanisms simultaneously.
Answer: Observe patterns, prioritise the most maladaptive ones, respond with empathy, avoid overwhelming the patient, and support gradual coping.
94
6 marksHow can a nurse support a patient using compensation in a healthy way?
Answer: Acknowledge strengths, encourage realistic goals, help them accept limitations without shame.
95
5 marksWhat is the difference between reaction formation and undoing?
Answer: Reaction formation = behaving opposite to true feeling. Undoing = performing a ritual to “reverse” a forbidden act.
96
7 marksCase: A patient with cancer says “I’ll beat this by thinking positively; negative people just give up.” Identify defence(s).
Answer: Possible reaction formation (overly optimistic to mask fear) and rationalisation.
97
6 marksHow do defence mechanisms relate to the concept of psychological safety?
Answer: They temporarily preserve psychological safety by reducing anxiety, but can undermine long‑term adjustment if overused.
98
5 marksList two defence mechanisms that are primarily reality‑distorting.
Answer: Denial, projection.
99
7 marksHow can a nurse use reflection to help a patient become aware of a defence mechanism?
Answer: Gently reflect observed patterns: “I notice when we talk about your diagnosis, you focus on the technical details. Sometimes that helps us avoid feeling scared. How are you feeling underneath?”
100
10 marksSummarise the importance of understanding defence mechanisms for a nursing professional, integrating theory, communication, and self‑care.
Answer: Understanding defence mechanisms enables nurses to interpret challenging behaviours as coping, respond therapeutically, foster adaptive strategies, and recognise their own defences to prevent burnout. It bridges psychological theory and compassionate, safe patient care.
